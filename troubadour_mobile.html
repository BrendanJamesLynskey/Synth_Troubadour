About Troubadour Song

Secular monophony • 12th–13th century • Occitania & France

The troubadours of Occitania and, in the north, the trouvères were poet-composers who sang in the vernacular — Occitan and Old French — rather than in liturgical Latin. Theirs was the great secular art of fin’amor, courtly love: song crafted for the courts of Aquitaine, Toulouse and Champagne, prizing the lady, longing and the ache of desire above all.

The songs took shape as the canso (the love-song), the dansa and lively estampida. Each is a single monophonic melody, flexible in rhythm — the poet sang the line while the bowed vielle held a drone beneath. Figures such as Bernart de Ventadorn, the Comtessa Beatriz de Dia, and the northern Adam de la Halle shaped a repertoire whose melodies still survive. This engine sings that lyric line with the shared FOF vocal-synthesis library (vocal-voices.js, the IRCAM CHANT method) — overlapping formant grains sung as vowels, a haunting solo voice — over a subtractive-synth vielle drone.

Where it sits in the lineage

Troubadour song is the secular, vernacular counterpart to sacred plainchant — a parallel monophonic tradition, but of the court and the garden rather than the abbey. Its melodies and its dance-songs fed directly into the instrumental forms of the Middle Ages, above all the whirling estampie.
